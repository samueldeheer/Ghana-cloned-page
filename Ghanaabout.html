Geography

Location & Landscape

Ghana, officially the Republic of Ghana, is a country in West Africa. It borders Côte d'Ivoire to the west, Burkina Faso to the north, and Togo to the east, while its southern coastline faces the Gulf of Guinea and the Atlantic Ocean.

The country covers 239,567 km² (92,497 sq mi), with landscapes ranging from coastal savannas in the south to dense tropical rainforests and open northern savanna. With a population of over 35 million, Ghana is the thirteenth-most populous country in Africa and the second-most populous in West Africa. Its capital and largest city is Accra.

History

From Ancient Kingdoms to Independence

The earliest kingdoms to emerge in Ghana were Bonoman in the south and the Kingdom of Dagbon in the north, with Bonoman established as far back as the 11th century. Over the following centuries, the Asante Empire and other Akan kingdoms rose to prominence in the south.

From the 15th century onward, the Portuguese Empire and later other European powers competed for trading rights in the region. By the 19th century, the British had established control of the coast. After more than a century of colonial resistance, the country's modern borders took shape, unifying four separate British colonial territories: the Gold Coast, Ashanti, the Northern Territories, and British Togoland — all brought together as an independent dominion within the Commonwealth of Nations.

"On 6 March 1957, Ghana became the first country in Sub-Saharan Africa to achieve independence."

Under President Kwame Nkrumah, Ghana went on to play a leading role in the decolonisation of Africa and the broader Pan-African movement.

People & Government

A Diverse and Democratic Nation

Ghana is a multi-ethnic nation with diverse linguistic and religious communities. The Akan are the largest ethnic group, though they represent a plurality rather than a majority. The majority of Ghanaians are Christians (71.3%), nearly a fifth are Muslims, and the remainder practice traditional faiths or no religion.

Politically, Ghana is a unitary constitutional democracy headed by a president who serves as both head of state and head of government. The country is recognised as one of Africa's more stable democracies, ranking seventh in the 2022 Ibrahim Index of African Governance and fifth in the 2024 Fragile States Index.

On the international stage, Ghana is a founding member of the Non-Aligned Movement and the African Union, and holds membership in the United Nations, ECOWAS, the Group of 24, and the Commonwealth of Nations.

Name & Identity

The Empire of Ghana

The name Ghana originates from Wagadu, a West African empire that existed from the 3rd to 12th centuries. Arab traders involved in the trans-Saharan trade referred to Wagadu as "Ghana" — a title derived from Kaya Maghan, meaning ruler of gold, used by Wagadu's kings.

Adoption in Precolonial Scholarship

The earliest attempt to connect the peoples of the Gold Coast with ancient Ghana was made by Reverend J. B. Anaman around the turn of the 20th century. Drawing on earlier interpretations of Arabic geographical sources, Anaman argued for a historical connection between the Gold Coast and the ancient kingdom.

The theory gained wider traction through the teachings of Reverend W. T. Balmer between 1907 and 1911, who taught that the Akan people had migrated from the ancient kingdom near the Upper Niger. Though the hypothesis lacked linguistic and historical evidence, it became influential among educated elites and nationalist intellectuals.

It was later popularised in schools — particularly at Achimota in the 1920s — and further advanced through the academic writings of J. B. Danquah, who used Arabic and French translations to claim that the term "Ghana" was a corruption of Akane or Akana, connecting it to the ancient region of Akkad.
