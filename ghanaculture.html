Kente & Textiles

Woven in Gold and Glory

Kente cloth is Ghana's most recognisable cultural export — a hand-woven textile of silk and cotton, created on a narrow loom by the Akan and Ewe peoples. Each strip is roughly four inches wide and woven separately before being sewn together into a full cloth.

Originally reserved exclusively for royalty and sacred ceremonies, kente is now worn across the world as a symbol of African pride and heritage. Every colour and geometric pattern carries meaning — gold for royalty and wealth, green for renewal, red for sacrifice and struggle, black for spiritual energy and maturity.

The town of Bonwire, near Kumasi in the Ashanti Region, remains the heartland of kente weaving, where master weavers pass the craft from father to son across generations.

Music & Dance

From the Fontomfrom to Highlife

Music is the heartbeat of Ghanaian life. The ancient Fontomfrom royal drums of the Ashanti were once reserved solely for the Asantehene — their thunderous sound signalling the presence of royalty. Today, drumming and dancing are woven into every community gathering, funeral, and festival.

In the 20th century, Ghana gave the world Highlife — a genre that blended traditional Akan melodies with Western brass instruments and guitar. Legends like E.T. Mensah and Ebo Taylor defined an era. The tradition continues today through Afrobeats and Azonto, the Ghanaian dance style that swept the continent.

The kpanlogo rhythm from the Ga people of Accra, the agbadza of the Ewe, and the royal adowa dance of the Akan — each ethnic group carries its own distinct rhythmic language.

Food & Cuisine

Shared Bowls,
Shared Lives

Ghanaian cuisine is communal by nature. Meals are rarely eaten alone — food is a reason to gather, and gathering is a reason to cook. The foundational staple is fufu, pounded cassava and plantain served with rich soups: light soup, groundnut soup, or palm nut soup.

Jollof rice — Ghana's deeply spiced, tomato-based rice dish — is a point of national pride and a subject of friendly rivalry with Nigeria. Banku and kenkey, both fermented corn doughs, are eaten with grilled tilapia, pepper sauce, and shito — Ghana's bold, smoky black chilli paste.

Street food culture thrives at every corner. Kelewele — spiced fried plantain — is perhaps the most beloved snack, sold at nighttime markets under the warm glow of lanterns across Accra and Kumasi.

Festivals

Celebration as
Sacred Duty

Ghana's festivals are not performances for tourists — they are living ceremonies that reconnect communities with their ancestors, their history, and each other. The Homowo festival of the Ga people, whose name translates as "hooting at hunger," celebrates the end of a historic famine with feasting, drumming, and the pouring of libations for the dead.

The Aboakyir deer-hunting festival in Winneba, the Odwira purification festival of the Akan, and the spectacular Panafest — the Pan-African Historical Theatre Festival held in Cape Coast — each draw Ghanaians home from across the diaspora.

The Durbar of Chiefs, held during major festivals, is among the most visually magnificent ceremonies in Africa — paramount chiefs carried in palanquins, dressed in kente and gold, surrounded by the sound of fontomfrom drums and the cheers of thousands.

Adinkra Symbols

Adinkra are visual symbols created by the Akan people that represent concepts, aphorisms, and proverbs. Originally used on cloth and pottery, they are today found in architecture, logos, and art across Ghana and the world.

Language

Akwaaba —
You Are Welcome

Ghana is a linguistically rich nation with over 80 languages and dialects. English serves as the official language and the medium of education and government, a legacy of British colonisation — but it is the indigenous languages that carry the true texture of Ghanaian identity.

Twi (a dialect of Akan) is the most widely spoken local language, used as a lingua franca across much of southern Ghana. The word Akwaaba — meaning "welcome" — is one of the most recognised Twi words in the world, seen on signs at Kotoka International Airport and heard in homes across the country.

Other major languages include Ewe, Ga, Dagbani, and Hausa. Ghanaian proverbs — called ebe in Twi — are considered a form of oral literature, used by elders to teach, counsel, and preserve wisdom across generations.
